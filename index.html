SUGAR PRODUCTION

Sugarcane, a tall perennial grass, is the primary source of sugar production globally, accounting for nearly 80% of the world’s sugar output. The process of converting sugarcane into sugar is complex and involves several stages, from harvesting to refining. Below is an overview of the steps involved in sugar production from sugarcane.

 

Cultivation and Harvesting

Sugarcane cultivation typically occurs in tropical and subtropical regions. It is grown from cuttings, and after about 10-18 months of growth, the cane reaches maturity and is ready for harvesting. Harvesting can be done manually with machetes or using mechanical harvesters. In some regions, burning the fields before harvesting is practiced to remove excess leaves, though this method is being phased out due to environmental concerns.

Transportation to the Mill

Once harvested, sugarcane must be processed quickly as it begins to lose its sucrose content shortly after being cut. The cane is transported to nearby mills via trucks, tractors, or railways. Upon arrival at the mill, the cane is weighed and prepared for crushing.

Extraction of Juice

The first step in the milling process is the extraction of juice from the sugarcane. The cane is washed and fed into a series of crushing mills, which are heavy-duty rollers that squeeze the juice out of the cane. The juice extracted contains water, sucrose, and impurities like dirt and fibrous material (bagasse). The remaining fibrous residue, known as bagasse, is often used as a biofuel to power the mill or for other industrial purposes.

Clarification

The raw juice extracted from the cane contains impurities that need to be removed. This is done through a clarification process, where the juice is treated with heat and lime (calcium oxide). The lime neutralizes the acidity and causes impurities to coagulate and settle at the bottom. The clear juice is then filtered, leaving behind the sediment known as mud, which is often recycled as fertilizer.

Evaporation

The clarified juice is concentrated into a syrup by evaporating most of the water. This is done using multiple-effect evaporators, which consist of a series of vessels where the juice is heated and water is removed in stages. The syrup produced is thick and contains about 60% sucrose.

Crystallization

The concentrated syrup undergoes crystallization in large vacuum pans. By controlling the temperature and pressure, sugar crystals begin to form. These crystals grow as more sucrose is deposited, and once they reach the desired size, the mixture of crystals and syrup (known as massecuite) is sent to centrifuges.

Separation and Drying

In the centrifuges, the crystals are separated from the remaining liquid, known as molasses. The molasses can either be sold as a by-product or used for further extraction. The separated sugar crystals are washed, dried, and then cooled to prevent clumping.

Refining

Raw sugar is typically refined to produce the white, granulated sugar found in households. In the refinery, raw sugar is dissolved, filtered, and subjected to further purification processes to remove remaining impurities and color. The refined sugar is then crystallized again, dried, and packaged for distribution.

Packaging and Distribution

The final sugar product is packaged into various forms such as granulated sugar, powdered sugar, or cubes and distributed to markets worldwide. The entire process from field to table is meticulously managed to ensure high-quality sugar production.

Sugar production from sugarcane is a vital industry in many countries, supporting livelihoods and economies while fulfilling a global demand for sweeteners. However, the process also raises environmental and social considerations, particularly concerning water usage, labor practices, and the impact on ecosystems
